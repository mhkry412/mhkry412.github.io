Why Stanene? A Case for Tinene

Stanene (2D tin) is predicted to be a topological insulator with edge states at room temperature — something even graphene cannot do. During my internship at BRIN, I ran DFT calculations on stanene using Quantum Espresso. The band structure shows a clear Dirac cone with strong spin-orbit coupling opening a nontrivial gap. What makes it exciting is the potential for dissipationless transport in a material that can be grown on standard substrates. Next step: investigating how strain and substrate interaction modify the topological phase.

DFT+U: When Approximations Need Fixing

Standard LDA/GGA functionals fail for strongly correlated systems (transition metal oxides, rare earths). DFT+U adds a Hubbard-like term to correct the self-interaction error. In my thesis work on hBN with defect variations, I compared PBE vs PBE+U band structures. The +U correction shifts defect states and changes the predicted optical transition energies significantly. Key takeaway: always test convergence in U — and be honest about the empirical nature of the parameter.

Reference: Anisimov et al., Phys. Rev. B 50, 16861 (1994)

Notes on Topological Invariants

A simple way to think about Z2 invariants: count how many times the Berry phase winds across the Brillouin zone. If it winds an odd number of times, you get a topological insulator. For inversion-symmetric crystals, it reduces to checking parity eigenvalues at TRIM points — the Fu-Kane formula. I wrote a small Python script to compute Z2 from Quantum Espresso output by post-processing the wavefunctions. It is not trivial to get right (gauge choices matter), but once it works, predicting topological phases becomes systematic.

2026-01-28
Graphene Oxide from Coconut Fiber

During my internship with G'nomac, we synthesized graphene oxide (GO) from coconut fiber biomass. The process: pyrolysis of coconut fiber, chemical oxidation via Hummers' method, then exfoliation. The yield was comparable to graphite-derived GO, with similar interlayer spacing (~0.8 nm from XRD). It is a reminder that advanced materials can come from agricultural waste — making quantum materials research more accessible and sustainable.
